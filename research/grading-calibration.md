## Executive Summary

Two non-professional graders can achieve ICC ≥ 0.75 ("good" reliability per Koo & Mae 2016) for CGC-scale comic grading with the right preparation. The key requirements are: (1) shared reference materials studied before any grading begins, (2) a minimum of 20–30 anchor-calibration comics graded jointly with structured disagreement discussion, and (3) blind independent grading of production comics followed by ICC verification. The primary risk is upward bias — the single most documented failure mode in collector grading. The most actionable resource is the CGC Grader Notes Guide (free online), augmented by the Official CGC Guide to Grading Comics book (~$30–50) and the Udemy course "Learn to Grade Comic Books" (~$15–20 on sale).

## 2. Grade Boundary Definitions

All boundary definitions below synthesize CGC, Overstreet, and community consensus. Measurements are the minimum physical criteria found across sources; where sources conflict, the more restrictive (lower) threshold is used.

### 2.1 The 25-Point Scale: Full Definitions with Boundary Criteria

**[CLAIM-GRADE-01]** The CGC 10.0 (Gem Mint) grade is effectively unreachable: "no evidence of any manufacturing or handling defects." Golden Age examples do not exist. This grade should never be assigned by amateur graders.  
**Confidence:** HIGH

**[CLAIM-GRADE-02]** Grades 9.8 (NM/M) and below: The 9.8 boundary is defined by "negligible" defects discoverable only under specific conditions (angled light, specific viewing angle). Specific allowances at 9.8:
- Spine ticks: ≤ 1/8" that are not visible looking straight on
- Bindery tears: ≤ 1/16" at spine top/bottom
- Spine rub: Running the length of the spine from shipping friction
- Miswraps: ≤ 1/16"
- Color rubs: Total area ≤ 1"
- Waviness: Subtle enough to press out during encapsulation

A 9.8 can contain 1–2 such defects; 3 only if "super minor." The moment a spine tick is visible in any light looking straight on, the grade drops to 9.6 or lower.  
**Source:** [src-009]  
**Confidence:** HIGH — sourced from detailed analysis of actual CGC 9.8 submissions.

**[CLAIM-GRADE-03]** 9.4 (NM) vs. 9.0 (VF/NM): This is the most commercially important boundary.

| Feature | 9.4 NM | 9.0 VF/NM |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| Max bend | 1/16" without color break | 1/8" without color break |
| Color break creases | Not permitted | Not permitted (minor bends only) |
| Ink reflectivity | Bright, high reflectivity | Slightly diminished reflectivity |
| Eye appeal | Nearly perfect | Good, minor detraction |
| Allowable defect count | ~5 minor defects | ~6 minor defects |
| Pages | Off-white to white | Off-white acceptable |

Key differentiator: At 9.4, the book must have bright colors and look nearly fresh. At 9.0, slight loss of reflectivity is acceptable and the bend tolerance doubles. A single spine tick that breaks color at a staple would typically drop a 9.4 to 9.2 or 9.0.  
**Source:** [src-006], [src-009], [src-010]  
**Confidence:** HIGH — corroborated by three independent sources.

**[CLAIM-GRADE-04]** 8.5 (VF+) vs. 8.0 (VF):

| Feature | 8.5 VF+ | 8.0 VF |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Classification | "VF with additional virtue" | Baseline Very Fine |
| Acceptable crease | Minor creases ≤ 3" without color break | Color breaks ≤ 1/4" acceptable |
| Defect count | ~7 allowed | ~7 allowed |
| Eye appeal | Perceptibly better than 8.0 | "Outstanding eye appeal" with minimal surface wear |
| Pages | Off-white to white | Off-white |

8.5 is essentially a premium 8.0 — same defect tolerance but better cumulative eye appeal. If a 8.0 book has one strong virtue that makes it look noticeably better, it goes to 8.5. The distinction is largely holistic.  
**Source:** [src-006], [src-011]  
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — the 8.5/8.0 boundary is notably holistic and harder to operationalize than others.

**[CLAIM-GRADE-05]** 7.0 (FN/VF) vs. 6.5 (FN+):

| Feature | 7.0 FN/VF | 6.5 FN+ |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Category | Above-average | Fine with enhancement |
| Creases | Minor to moderate; may have color-breaking creases | Moderate color breaks; tiny missing pieces allowed |
| Spine | Possible 1/4" spine split | Spine splits beginning |
| Colors | Moderate reduction in reflectivity | Noticeably faded |
| Defect count | More extensive wear | Fine range but better than 6.0 |

The 7.0/6.5 transition is the dividing line between "above-average" and "average/Fine" grades. Books at 7.0 are collectible; books at 6.5 are reading copies with obvious wear. A visible spine roll that also shows spine stress would typically move a book from 7.0 to 6.5.  
**Source:** [src-006], [src-012]  
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — boundaries in the Fine range are well-documented but require judgment on cumulative effect.

**[CLAIM-GRADE-06]** 4.0 (VG) vs. 3.0 (GD/VG):

| Feature | 4.0 VG | 3.0 GD/VG |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| Condition | Below-average, multiple moderate defects | Shows significant evidence of handling |
| Spine | Possible detachment at one staple | May have vertical creases; possible detachment |
| Reflectivity | Moderate to extreme reduction | Very low |
| Tape | 4.5 is the highest grade for any tape present | Tape common |
| Missing parts | 1/4" corner triangle missing possible at VG/FN | Larger missing pieces |

The 4.0/3.0 line is the transition between "still desirable" and "heavily read." A 4.0 maintains general readability and collectible appeal; 3.0 begins showing structural deterioration. The presence of tape (even small) drops a book to ≤ 4.5 automatically. Vertical cover creases and detached covers push into the 3.0 range.  
**Source:** [src-006], [src-013]  
**Confidence:** HIGH — low-grade criteria are less contested than high-grade.

## 3. Common Grading Biases and Corrections

### 3.1 Upward Bias (Wishful Thinking)

**Claim:** Upward bias is the single most documented grading failure mode. Inexperienced collectors routinely grade 1–2 full grade categories higher than professionals.  
**Evidence:** Multiple sources confirm: "Grading is the most subjective aspect of determining a comic's value, and graders must take care not to allow wishful thinking to influence what the eyes see." The collector community acknowledges that "inexperienced comic book graders often think a 'Very Fine' comic is 'mint.'"  
**Source:** [src-014], [src-015]  
**Confidence:** HIGH — consistent across multiple independent sources.

**Correction protocol:** Grade the comic before checking any price guide, before knowing what the market grade is, and before the other grader's grade. Write down the grade. Lock it in. Never revise upward after initial assessment.

### 3.2 Anchoring Bias

**Claim:** When two graders grade together or when one grader knows the other's grade first, the second grade anchors to the first, reducing variance artificially and inflating apparent agreement.  
**Evidence:** CGC's own process is explicitly designed to prevent this: "Collectibles are separated from submission paperwork so that graders don't know the submitter's identity." Each grader enters their grade independently into CGC's computer system before a consensus is reached. The anchoring problem is well-established in psychological literature on ordinal rating scales.  
**Source:** [src-016], [src-017]  
**Confidence:** HIGH — CGC's process design is direct evidence anchoring is a known problem.

**Correction protocol:** Always grade independently and record grade before discussing with the other grader. Never announce a grade verbally during a session before the other person has written theirs down.

### 3.3 Age/Vintage Bias

**Claim:** Graders sometimes unconsciously apply looser standards to older comics ("expected to be worn") or stricter standards to modern books.  
**Evidence:** The Nostomania grading guide explicitly states: "All items should receive equal consideration regardless of age — no special allowances for Golden Age comics." This instruction exists precisely because graders routinely give age-based allowances that are not part of the CGC standard.  
**Source:** [src-018]  
**Confidence:** HIGH — explicitly prohibited by published grading standards, implying it is a known issue.

**Correction protocol:** Grade every comic against the absolute scale, not relative to what "should" be expected for that era. The CGC scale is uniform across all years.

### 3.4 Key Issue / Value Bias

**Claim:** High-value key issues (first appearances, first printings) are systematically graded higher by graders who know their market value.  
**Evidence:** The CGC forum discussion on grading bias specifically raises this: "whether there is any bias when grading comics based on the graders personal taste/distaste toward the individual book, rarity, or character." This is acknowledged as a concern, though no controlled study on this specific bias for comics was found.  
**Source:** [src-019]  
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — community-acknowledged concern, not formally studied in this domain.

**Correction protocol:** Grade all comics without knowing their current market value. Remove any price guide annotations or CGC census data from the table before grading sessions. Randomize the order so key issues are interspersed with unremarkable books.

### 3.5 Familiarity / Title Bias

**Claim:** Graders familiar with a title grade it differently (higher or lower) than unfamiliar titles.  
**Evidence:** Same CGC forum source acknowledges the question of whether "graders who are fans of a particular title would grade it higher or lower than another title in similar condition."  
**Source:** [src-019]  
**Confidence:** LOW — acknowledged in community but no quantitative evidence found.

**Correction protocol:** Cover or tape over the title during calibration exercises. This is non-destructive and removes the stimulus.

### 3.6 Session Fatigue

**Claim:** Cognitive fatigue degrades grading accuracy after sustained sessions. The optimal session length for precise visual inspection tasks is approximately 45–90 minutes before a break is required.  
**Evidence:** Research on decision fatigue shows "errors rise as fatigue accumulates" and that "the probability of making high-value care decisions decreased later in the day." One cognitive fatigue study induced degradation with a 24-minute sustained attention task followed by error increases. However, a large-scale study on medical decision-making found "no credible evidence for decision fatigue" in expert practitioners, suggesting experienced graders may be more resilient.  
**Source:** [src-020], [src-021]  
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — general fatigue research applies; no comic-specific study found.

**Correction protocol:** Grade no more than 20–25 comics per 90-minute block. Take a 15-minute break between blocks. Grade high-value or ambiguous comics early in the session. Spread the full 70-comic dataset across 3–4 sessions over 2–3 days rather than one marathon session.

### 3.7 Contrast Effects

**Claim:** Grading a 9.8 immediately before a 9.4 makes the 9.4 look worse by contrast, while grading a 3.0 immediately before makes it look better.  
**Evidence:** This is a well-established phenomenon in ordinal rating scales (sequential contrast effects). No comic-specific study was found but the general literature on ordinal rating confirms this.  
**Source:** [src-022]  
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — general rating research, not comic-specific.

**Correction protocol:** Randomize the grading order. Do not sort comics by condition before grading. Sort only after all grades have been recorded.

## 4. Calibration Protocol for Two Non-Professional Graders

### 4.1 What Professional Training Looks Like

**Claim:** Professional comic graders at CGC are trained by: (a) extensive study of internal defect standards, (b) practice grading with immediate expert feedback, and (c) team consensus grading where each grader enters a grade independently before consensus.  
**Evidence:** CGC's described process: three graders examine each book, each enters an independent grade, consensus is reached. "If two graders rate a book at 7.0 and the Head Grader grades it at 7.5, the latter grade prevails." Training requires "significant labor" and "many years of practice" to fully develop the grader's eye.  
**Source:** [src-016], [src-023]  
**Confidence:** HIGH.

**Claim:** No formal public CGC certification program exists for independent graders.  
**Evidence:** CGC forum post on "How do I become certified to grade comics?" received responses confirming CGC does not offer a public grader certification path.  
**Source:** [src-024]  
**Confidence:** HIGH.

### 4.2 Recommended Calibration Exercises

The following protocol is based on the research synthesis. It is designed to get two non-professional graders to ICC ≥ 0.75 within a 1–2 day preparation period.

**Phase 0: Pre-Study (Each grader independently, ~4–6 hours)**

1. Read the CGC Grader Notes Guide online in full. Work through all 7 defect classifications and all 109 defect types. Study the photographs.
2. Watch the Udemy course "Learn to Grade Comic Books" (8+ hours; use 1.5x speed to cover in ~5 hours). The course uses 27 actual CGC-graded books to illustrate every grade level.
3. Study the CGC grading scale page and the Overstreet grade boundary definitions. Memorize the broad grade categories and their characteristic defect profiles.
4. Compile a personal defect checklist — a laminated card listing the 7 defect categories and the boundary measurements for each major grade transition.

**Phase 1: Anchor Calibration (Both graders together, ~3 hours)**

Purpose: Establish shared reference points using comics with known or agreed-upon grades.

1. Select 10–15 anchor comics spanning the full grade range: aim for at least one in each category (NM, VF, FN, VG, GD, PR). These should be comics whose condition is clear and unambiguous.
2. Grade each anchor independently, without discussion, and write down the grade.
3. Reveal grades simultaneously (not sequentially — prevents anchoring).
4. For every comic where grades differ by > 0.5 points, conduct a structured discussion:
   - Each grader identifies which specific defect(s) drove their grade
   - Compare defect lists
   - Measure any relevant dimensions (crease length, bend size)
   - Reach consensus on what the grade should be and why
5. After discussion, write a brief "decision rule" for the class of defect that caused the disagreement.

**Phase 2: Progressive Calibration (Both graders together, ~2 hours)**

Purpose: Narrow the calibration from category-level to grade-point level.

1. Round A: Grade 10 extreme-case comics (obvious 9.8 vs. obvious 2.0 or lower). Expect near-perfect agreement. This builds confidence and confirms shared vocabulary.
2. Round B: Grade 10 moderate-case comics in the VF range (7.5–8.5). This is where most disagreement will surface.
3. Round C: Grade 10 ambiguous boundary comics: a deliberate 9.4-vs-9.0 pair, an 8.5-vs-8.0 pair, a 7.0-vs-6.5 pair.
4. After each round, calculate percent agreement (grades within 0.5 of each other = agreement). If agreement drops below 80%, revisit the decision rules from Phase 1.

**Phase 3: Blind Production Grading (Both graders independently)**

This is the actual grading session for the dataset.

1. Grade all 70 comics independently, without communication about grades.
2. Record grades to one decimal place on the CGC scale.
3. After all 70 are graded, reveal grades to each other.
4. Compute ICC.

### 4.3 How Many Rounds Before ICC Stabilizes?

**Claim:** For two raters grading a continuous/ordinal scale, ICC typically stabilizes after 2–3 calibration rounds using anchor examples with structured feedback, based on the general inter-rater reliability training literature.  
**Evidence:** The Texas childhood trauma research network study achieved κ = 0.877 after a structured two-day in-person workshop plus two 20-minute practice sessions with trainer feedback. The occupation-based coaching study achieved ICC = 0.867–0.999 after three-phase training with ongoing calibration. The literature consistently shows ICC stabilizes after a finite number of anchor-calibration rounds rather than requiring continuous practice for simple rating scales.  
**Source:** [src-025], [src-026]  
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — research context is not comic grading, but general pattern applies.

## 5. Inter-Rater Reliability Measurement

### 5.1 Which ICC Model to Use

**Claim:** For Sean and Marcus grading the same 70 comics, the correct model is ICC(3,1): Two-Way Mixed-Effects, Absolute Agreement, Single Measures.  
**Evidence:** ICC model selection logic from Koo & Mae 2016:
- Use ICC(3,1) when "the selected raters are the only raters of interest" and results do not need to generalize to other raters
- Use ICC(2,1) when raters are a "random sample from a larger population" you want to generalize to

Since Sean and Marcus are the specific raters of interest (not a sample of all possible raters), ICC(3,1) is correct. The "absolute agreement" variant is appropriate because actual grade differences (not just correlation) matter for dataset validity.  
**Source:** [src-027]  
**Confidence:** HIGH.

### 5.2 Computing ICC in Python

```python
import pingouin as pg
import pandas as pd

# Data format: one row per (comic, rater) pair
data = pd.DataFrame({
    'Subject': list(range(70)) * 2,
    'Rater': ['Sean'] * 70 + ['Marcus'] * 70,
    'Score': sean_grades + marcus_grades  # each a list of 70 floats
})

result = pg.intraclass_corr(
    data=data,
    targets='Subject',
    raters='Rater',
    ratings='Score',
    nan_policy='omit'
)

# ICC(3,1) row = 'ICC3' in pingouin output
icc31 = result[result['Type'] == 'ICC3']
print(icc31[['ICC', 'CI95%', 'pval']])
```

Always report the 95% confidence interval, not just the point estimate.  
**Source:** [src-028]  
**Confidence:** HIGH.

### 5.3 Minimum Sample Size

**Claim:** 70 comics is near the lower bound for stable ICC estimates with two raters; expect wide confidence intervals but the point estimate will be meaningful.  
**Evidence:** Koo & Mae (2016) recommend "at least 30 heterogeneous samples" as a minimum. The general inter-rater reliability literature recommends 50–100 subjects for stable ICC estimates. A formal power calculation for ICC with two raters targeting power = 0.80, H₀: ICC ≤ 0.60, H₁: ICC ≥ 0.75 yields approximately 50–60 subjects needed. 70 comics provides slightly more than this minimum.  
**Source:** [src-027], [src-029]  
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — the 70-comic dataset is marginally sufficient; wide CI should be expected and reported.

**Critical note:** For stable ICC, the 70 comics must span a wide grade range (heterogeneous). Grading 70 comics that are all 9.0–9.8 will yield an ICC with artificially poor reliability even if both graders are perfectly calibrated, because there is too little variance between subjects. Ensure the dataset covers the full 0.5–10.0 range.

### 5.4 Interpreting ICC Results

| ICC Value | Interpretation | Action |
|-----------|----------------|--------|
| ≥ 0.90 | Excellent | Proceed; dataset is publication-quality |
| 0.75–0.90 | Good | Proceed; meets the target threshold |
| 0.60–0.74 | Moderate | Review divergent cases; add calibration round |
| < 0.60 | Poor | Full recalibration required before proceeding |

### 5.5 When ICC < 0.75: Diagnostic Protocol

**Claim:** If ICC falls below 0.75, the most effective remediation is to identify the specific grade range or defect type causing disagreement rather than regrading all 70 comics.  
**Evidence:** The research on rater training shows: "a low ICC could relate to lack of variability among subjects, small number of subjects, and small number of raters." Beyond these structural issues, specific disagreement patterns reveal the actual problem.

Protocol when ICC < 0.75:
1. Plot Sean's grades vs. Marcus's grades as a scatter plot. Look for: (a) systematic offset (one grader consistently grades higher — upward bias), (b) fan-shaped scatter (disagreement increases at high grades), or (c) clustered disagreement in a specific range.
2. Identify all comics where grades diverge by > 1.0 point. Re-examine those comics jointly.
3. If the divergence is systematic (e.g., Sean consistently grades 0.5 higher than Marcus), apply a calibration correction rather than regrading — the bias is known and can be modeled.
4. Add a third calibration round using only the disagreement comics. Reach consensus grades. Compute ICC again.  
**Source:** [src-027], [src-025]  
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — protocol is derived from general inter-rater reliability literature.

### 5.6 Weighted Kappa as Alternative

**Claim:** Weighted kappa (quadratic weights) is an appropriate alternative to ICC for ordinal scale data and can be computed if you treat the 25 CGC grade points as ordered categories.  
**Evidence:** Weighted kappa is "the standard measure of inter-rater reliability for ordinal scales because it respects the ordering of categories and gives partial credit for near-agreements." For the CGC 25-point scale, quadratic weights would appropriately penalize a 9.8/9.0 disagreement more than a 9.8/9.6 disagreement.  
**Source:** [src-030]  
**Confidence:** HIGH.

However, ICC is preferable here because: (a) the CGC scale can be treated as approximately continuous (25 ordered points with roughly equal spacing), (b) ICC handles continuous data more efficiently with small samples, and (c) ICC(3,1) directly tests the quantity of interest (absolute agreement between two fixed raters).

## 6. Practical Grading Session Design

### 6.1 Physical Setup

**Claim:** Optimal grading requires: (a) a dedicated desk lamp producing bright diffuse light, (b) a clean white or neutral surface, (c) clean dry hands (cotton gloves optional), and (d) a 10x–12x loupe or magnifying glass for close defect inspection.  
**Evidence:** Professional consensus: "You need good lighting such as a desk lamp." Moving the book "at different angles under the light allows you to view the gloss and also look for defects like light creases, folds, stains, dimples or other types of damage." A 10x–12x magnifying glass "is sufficient to cover grading needs." Spine stress lines "sometimes cannot be viewed under normal light" and require angling the book.  
**Source:** [src-031], [src-032]  
**Confidence:** HIGH.

**Claim:** A UV/black light is useful for detecting restoration (color touch, tape residue, chemical cleaning) but is not required for condition grading.  
**Evidence:** Multiple sources confirm black light reveals "color touched areas" and tape residue that is invisible under white light. CGC uses black light as part of their standard examination for restoration detection.  
**Source:** [src-033]  
**Confidence:** HIGH — UV light detects restoration, not standard wear.

Optional but recommended equipment: a ruler or calipers for measuring crease length and spine splits at ambiguous boundaries.

### 6.2 The Examination Sequence

The following sequence is derived from multiple grading guides and represents professional standard practice:

1. **First impression** (book closed, 5 seconds): Note major issues immediately visible — cover stains, large tears, extreme wear. Set a preliminary grade range (e.g., "this is somewhere in the Fine range").

2. **Cover examination** (30–60 seconds): Move the book under the lamp at multiple angles. Check:
   - Front cover: gloss/reflectivity level, stains, color rubs, creases, tears
   - Spine: spine ticks, stress lines, spine roll, color breaks at staples
   - Corners: blunting level (sharp = NM; rounded = VG range)
   - Back cover: same as front

3. **Spine and staple inspection** (15–30 seconds):
   - Examine spine for splits (measure if present)
   - Check staples from outside: rust, tears around staples, staple detachment
   - Check staples from inside: crimping (should be flat, not raised)

4. **Interior examination** (30–60 seconds):
   - Page color: white = best; cream = NM range; off-white = VF range; tan = FN/VG range; brown = low grade
   - Page texture: feel for brittleness (any brittleness = ≤ 1.8)
   - Check centerfold attachment: should be attached at both staples for NM; slight looseness = VF/FN
   - Scan interior pages for stamps, writing, clipped coupons, tears, water damage

5. **Grade assignment**: Start from the "ceiling" — what grade does this book look like at first glance? Then work down by noting each defect. Assign the lowest grade supported by the worst defects present, not the average. The worst single defect determines the ceiling; accumulation of smaller defects can push the grade further down.

6. **Record the grade** in writing before any discussion.

### 6.3 Session Structure Recommendations

**Claim:** For two-person grading sessions, the optimal block length is 20–25 comics per 90-minute session, with 15-minute breaks between sessions and spread across 2–3 days.  
**Evidence:** While no comic-specific fatigue study was found, the general cognitive fatigue literature shows accuracy degradation after sustained attention tasks of 45–90 minutes. Grading requires sustained visual attention and rapid ordinal judgment. The 70-comic dataset should comfortably fit into 3–4 sessions of 90 minutes each.  
**Source:** [src-020]  
**Confidence:** MEDIUM.

**Recommended session plan:**
- Session 1 (Day 1): Calibration exercise (Phases 1–2 from Section 4.2), ~20 anchor comics
- Session 2 (Day 1 or 2): Production grading, comics 1–25, independent/blind
- Session 3 (Day 2): Production grading, comics 26–50, independent/blind
- Session 4 (Day 2 or 3): Production grading, comics 51–70, compute ICC

**Claim:** Comics should be graded in random order, not sorted by condition.  
**Evidence:** Sorting by condition introduces contrast effects (see Section 3.7) and may create category anchoring where graders know "we're in the 9.0 range right now." Random order is standard in psychometric rating studies.  
**Source:** [src-022]  
**Confidence:** MEDIUM.

### 6.4 Recording Form Design

Each grader should use a paper grading form with the following fields per comic:
- Comic ID (pre-assigned, not the title/issue — to reduce familiarity bias)
- First impression grade range (circle one: NM / VF / FN / VG / GD / lower)
- Defects noted (list the defect type and location: e.g., "spine tick, top staple, ~1/16", color break")
- Final grade (to nearest 0.5 or 0.2 at high grades)
- Confidence level (1–5, where 1 = very uncertain, 5 = very confident)
- Flag if borderline between two grades (yes/no)

The confidence rating and borderline flag allow post-hoc analysis of which comics drove ICC reduction.

## 7. Open Questions and Items for Follow-Up

1. **CGC Sample Images**: CGC does not appear to publish per-grade sample images in an accessible format. The Udemy course is the closest practical substitute. If budget allows, submitting 2–3 comics to CGC before the calibration phase would provide ground-truth anchors.

2. **Borderline Case Protocol**: The research does not address how to handle comics that are genuine 0.5-point borderlines (e.g., truly between 9.0 and 9.2). A protocol decision should be made before production grading: recommend defaulting to the lower grade for borderlines to be conservative, and flagging them for potential consensus grading.

3. **Page Quality Grading**: CGC also grades page quality separately (W, OW, OW-W, C). This axis has not been addressed in this research. Determine whether the AI pipeline requires page quality annotation and if so, add a separate calibration exercise for page quality.

4. **Verified Graded Anchors**: If any of the 70 comics are already CGC-graded (or can be cross-referenced against community census data), use those as known-grade anchors in calibration. This provides ground truth that the Udemy course approximates but does not replace.
